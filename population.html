Population Change

Population and demographics is the other biggest factor when it comes to housing affordability. A common misconception is that a large influx of overseas immigrants decreases the supply of housing, this being spread mostly by right-wing and or people that are anti-immigrants to push their agenda. Part of this page is to disprove that theory.

Figure 1. Median House Price for Each Region

The historical median price for established houses for each capital city and rest of the state over time.

Prices grow steadily and linearly from 2003 to 2020, except for Sydney, Canberra and Melbourne which are consistently more expensive and less predictable compared to the other regions. Then COVID hit, as indicated by the dashed red line, raising the demand for housing (especially with less people per household) and median prices at an unprecedented rate. This has led to a larger separation in median price between the most and least populated regions as shown by the red arrow. A notable drop between 2008-2010 was caused by the Great Recession and the resulting economic downturn and housing market crash. This contrasts the spike caused by COVID, as banks had to sell houses for lower prices to recover losses and people couldn’t get loans, leading to a drop in the supply and demand.

Figure 2. Median House Prices for all of Australia

The historical median price for established houses averaged for all state capitals, outer regions, and all regions in Australia over time.

Figure 2 only shows the averages for the capital cities and the outer regions of each state, with the Australia line being the average of the two. The same drop in 2008 and spike in 2020 can be seen as in Figure 1, just less cluttered. Both inner city and regional prices are affected by both events, but the capital cities more so. Normal inflation is more easily visible in Figure 2 than Figure 1, with the gradient changing between each event as shown by the black dashed lines.
